10. Monopoly + Negative Externalities

Based on Dr. Said's Figures 10, 10-a, 10-b (slides pp. 47-56)

Two Opposing Distortions

When a monopoly exists alongside negative externalities, there are two distortions pulling in opposite directions:

  1. Monopoly: Underproduces (restricts output below competitive level to raise price)
  2. Negative Externality: Overproduces (ignores external cost, produces too much)

Three Scenarios

Perfect Offset (Fig 10) Monopolist Overproduces (Fig 10-a) Monopolist Underproduces (Fig 10-b)
$10
Figure 10: Perfect Offset (q_monopoly = q_efficient)

The monopolist produces q1 where MR = MPC. By coincidence, this equals the efficient output where MSB = MSC (at E3). The two distortions perfectly cancel out.

Result: No government intervention needed! The market is already at efficiency.

This is the "second best solution" - the monopolistic distortion offsets the externality distortion.

Figure 10-a: Monopolist Still Overproduces (q_monopoly > q_efficient)

The MEC is large enough that the efficient output (q2, where MSB=MSC) is less than the monopolist's output (q1, where MR=MPC).

Solution: Corrective tax needed, but LESS than under perfect competition. The monopoly already restricts some output.

Figure 10-b: Monopolist Underproduces (q_monopoly < q_efficient)

The MEC is small, so the efficient output (q*) is greater than the monopolist's output (q1).

Solution: A subsidy (not a tax!) is needed to encourage the monopolist to increase output to q*. The monopoly restriction is the dominant problem.

First-best solution: Break up the monopoly AND impose corrective tax simultaneously.

Second-best solution: If breaking up monopoly is infeasible, allow it to offset the externality naturally.
